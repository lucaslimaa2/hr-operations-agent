# Performance Improvement Policy

## When a PIP is appropriate

A Performance Improvement Plan is a structured, time-bounded intervention used when an employee's sustained performance falls materially below the expectations of their role and level, and when informal coaching has not produced the necessary change. It is neither a routine performance-management tool nor a procedural gateway to termination — it is a serious intervention used in a small number of cases, and one that the company is committed to running in good faith.

A PIP is appropriate when three conditions are met. First, the performance gap is **specific and observable** — there are concrete examples of work product, behavior, or results that fall below expectations, and they can be described in writing without ambiguity. Second, the gap is **material** — it affects the employee's ability to perform the core responsibilities of the role, not a peripheral skill or stylistic preference. Third, the gap has **persisted despite feedback** — the manager has had at least one prior conversation, documented in 1:1 notes or in writing, identifying the issue and setting expectations.

A PIP is not appropriate in several circumstances that managers and HRBPs occasionally confuse with underperformance. It is not appropriate as a response to a manager-employee personality conflict where the employee's actual deliverables are sound. It is not appropriate as a way to push an employee out of a role the manager wants to backfill with a preferred candidate. It is not appropriate during an active complaint of harassment or discrimination filed by the employee, until the investigation has resolved. It is not appropriate as a response to an employee's exercise of a protected right (taking parental leave, raising a workplace-safety concern, filing a workers' comp claim, organizing activity, etc.) — initiating a PIP in proximity to any of these is a textbook retaliation pattern and triggers Legal review before the PIP may begin.

## Pre-PIP coaching expectations

Before any PIP is opened, the manager is expected to have completed a documented coaching cycle of at least 30 days. The minimum components: a written summary of the performance concern shared with the employee, specific examples grounded in observed work, the expectations the employee is being held to, and a commitment from both sides to follow up. The follow-ups are weekly 1:1 conversations with notes captured by the manager.

The HR Business Partner is involved from the coaching stage forward, not introduced for the first time at the PIP. This is partly so the HRBP can advise the manager on framing and consistency with how other employees on the team are being managed, and partly so the HRBP has a credible basis to assert that the PIP, when it begins, is the continuation of a process rather than the start of one.

## PIP structure

A standard PIP runs **30, 60, or 90 days** depending on the seniority of the role and the complexity of the work being measured. Individual contributors below L4 typically run 30- or 60-day plans; managers, senior ICs, and Director-and-above roles typically run 60- or 90-day plans, because the work cycles in which performance is measurable are longer.

Each PIP contains the same components regardless of length:

- A **statement of the performance gap**, written in concrete terms and grounded in the prior coaching cycle.
- **Specific, measurable goals** that, if met, will indicate the gap is closed. Goals are observable outcomes, not effort metrics. "Close five enterprise opportunities at $X ARR" is a PIP goal; "make more sales calls" is not.
- A **check-in cadence**, typically weekly, with written notes from each check-in capturing progress and any obstacles raised by the employee.
- A **statement of consequences** — what happens if the goals are met (PIP closes, employee returns to standard performance management), what happens if they are partially met (potential extension), and what happens if they are not met (termination is a possible outcome).
- The **resources available** to the employee — coaching, training, mentorship, redistribution of workload, removal of obstacles the employee has identified.
- A **signed acknowledgment** by the employee that they have received and read the document, distinct from agreement with its contents.

## Documentation requirements

Every PIP produces a written document, signed by the employee, the manager, and the HRBP, stored in the employee's personnel file. The document is the controlling artifact — verbal modifications to the plan during the PIP period are not binding unless captured in a written addendum signed by all three parties.

Weekly check-in notes are captured by the manager and confirmed in writing with the employee, typically as a follow-up message after the 1:1 summarizing what was discussed. This creates a contemporaneous record. The cumulative record of check-in notes is reviewed at the midpoint and the end of the PIP period and is referenced in the closeout decision.

The closeout decision — successful completion, extension, or termination — is documented in writing with reference to the original goals and the check-in record. The HRBP reviews and counter-signs the closeout to confirm the decision is consistent with how comparable cases have been handled.

## Manager responsibilities

The manager owns the substance of the PIP from start to finish. This includes drafting the initial document (in collaboration with the HRBP but not delegated to them), running the weekly check-ins, providing genuine support and coaching during the period, evaluating progress honestly, and delivering the closeout outcome to the employee in person.

The manager is responsible for setting the employee up to succeed during the PIP period — clearing obstacles where possible, providing access to training or mentorship the employee needs, and being available for ad-hoc questions outside the weekly cadence. Managers who treat a PIP as a paper exercise leading to a predetermined termination outcome are the single most common source of post-termination wrongful-termination claims, regardless of jurisdiction.

The manager is also responsible for confidentiality. The fact of a PIP, its contents, and the outcome are confidential to the employee, the manager, the HRBP, and the limited set of people in the management chain who have a legitimate need to know.

## HR Business Partner responsibilities

The HRBP is the procedural counterweight to the manager. The HRBP reviews the draft PIP for fairness, clarity, and consistency with how comparable cases have been handled on the team and across the company. The HRBP advises the manager on goal-setting (specifically: are these goals genuinely achievable for an employee performing at standard?), on tone, and on documentation discipline.

The HRBP attends the PIP-opening conversation and is available to the employee throughout the period for confidential conversations about the process — distinct from the substance of the work, which is the manager's domain. The HRBP signs off on the closeout decision and is responsible for ensuring that the outcome is consistent with the company's prior practice in similar cases.

## Outcomes

The three possible outcomes at the end of a PIP are: **successful completion** (the employee returns to standard performance management, with the PIP and its outcome documented in the file but no further action); **extension** (an additional 30 days, used sparingly and only where the employee has shown meaningful progress but has not fully met the goals — extensions are not the norm and must be approved by the HRBP and the manager's skip-level); and **termination** (the employee has not met the goals and the employment relationship ends). 

In all three outcomes, the documentation discipline of the prior period is what makes the outcome defensible. The closeout decision is communicated to the employee in person, with the HRBP present.

## Country-specific notes

### France (FR)

A PIP in France has specific legal implications that managers operating under a US-centric framing routinely underestimate. French employment law does not recognize "performance improvement plan" as a defined legal instrument, but a manager-initiated process documenting performance concerns and leading to dismissal is treated by French courts as part of the **licenciement pour motif personnel** procedure, and the standards of proof and procedural fairness that apply to that procedure attach to the PIP documentation retrospectively. The motif personnel — the personal cause for dismissal — must be **réel et sérieux** (real and serious) and supported by the documentary record. Generic, subjective, or shifting goals will not survive Conseil de prud'hommes review.

Where a CSE (Comité Social et Économique) exists, consultation obligations may apply depending on company size and the nature of the planned dismissal procedure. The HRBP confirms scope before any termination decision is communicated to the employee. The pre-dismissal entretien préalable — a mandatory pre-dismissal interview with a five-business-day minimum notice and the employee's right to be accompanied — is required regardless of the prior PIP. The PIP record does not replace the entretien préalable; it supports it.

### Germany (DE)

PIP documentation is the foundation of a defensible **verhaltensbedingte Kündigung** (conduct-based termination) under the Kündigungsschutzgesetz, but it is not by itself sufficient. German labor law requires that conduct-based dismissal be preceded by at least one — typically more than one — formal written warning (**Abmahnung**), which gives the employee notice of the specific conduct deficiency, identifies the consequences of repetition, and creates the contemporaneous record that the Arbeitsgericht will examine if the termination is challenged.

A PIP is best understood, in the German context, as a structured documentation mechanism that produces the Abmahnung record alongside coaching. The PIP document itself does not substitute for an Abmahnung — the formal written warning must be issued and acknowledged as a distinct instrument when specific conduct triggers it. Where a Betriebsrat (works council) exists, BetrVG §102 consultation is required before any termination notice is issued, and the Betriebsrat will examine the PIP and Abmahnung record. Performance-based dismissal under the **personenbedingte Kündigung** ground (rather than conduct-based) has a separate and high evidentiary threshold and is not the typical PIP outcome.
