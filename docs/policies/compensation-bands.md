# Compensation Bands

## Band philosophy

The compensation band framework exists to deliver three outcomes simultaneously: pay that is internally equitable across teams and geographies, pay that is competitive against the external markets in which the company hires, and pay decisions that scale without becoming a CHRO bottleneck. The framework is the mechanism by which hiring managers can make confident offers within an envelope the company has already deliberated on.

Bands are guardrails, not targets. The midpoint of each band represents the company's calibrated view of competitive pay for a fully-performing employee at the level; the band width allows differentiation for tenure-in-level, exceptional performance, and scarce skills. Most employees at a given level are expected to land between the 40th and 70th percentile of their band. Placement at the band ceiling is reserved for employees with sustained exceptional performance and is reviewed annually for justification.

Bands are confidential to managers, HR, and the People team. They are not published company-wide. Individual employees are entitled, on request, to know their own band and where they sit within it; this is consistent with pay-transparency requirements that have come into force in California, Colorado, New York, and across the EU under the 2023 Pay Transparency Directive.

## Level framework

The framework spans seven levels (L1 through L7) across both the individual-contributor and management tracks. The two tracks are pay-equivalent at each level above L3 — a Staff Engineer (L4 IC) and an Engineering Manager (L4 management) are compensated within the same band, and movement between tracks is supported without the implicit pay penalty that asymmetric frameworks create.

| Level | IC track | Management track | Indicative US base range (USD) | Total comp character |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| L1 | Associate | — | $60,000–$85,000 | Mostly base; minimal equity |
| L2 | Mid | — | $85,000–$120,000 | Base + small equity grant |
| L3 | Senior | — | $120,000–$160,000 | Base + meaningful equity |
| L4 | Staff | Manager | $160,000–$210,000 | Base + significant equity + variable |
| L5 | Senior Staff | Senior Manager | $200,000–$260,000 | Base + significant equity + variable |
| L6 | Principal | Director | $240,000–$320,000 | Equity-weighted total comp |
| L7 | Senior Principal | VP | $300,000–$400,000+ | Equity-weighted; Board-reviewed |

The ranges above are anchored to the company's primary US market (Bay Area / New York). All other geographies are derived from these through the geo-adjustment described below. Variable compensation (annual bonus for non-sales roles, commission for sales roles) is layered on top of base per the role-specific incentive plan and is not represented in the base ranges.

## How bands are set

Bands are set annually by the People team's compensation function in partnership with the CFO and approved by the Compensation Committee of the Board. The inputs are: three external benchmark surveys (a primary survey for the technology sector, a sector-specific survey for the company's vertical, and a geography-specific survey for non-US markets), the company's own offer-acceptance and regrettable-attrition data from the prior 12 months, and a competitive-positioning decision (typically: pay at the 60th–65th percentile of the primary benchmark for non-sales roles; pay at the 50th–75th percentile of the OTE benchmark for sales roles).

The annual band refresh is completed in January, with new bands effective February 1. Bands are also reviewed mid-year if the relevant external market has moved materially — defined as a 5%+ shift in benchmark midpoints. Mid-year refreshes have happened twice in the last four years (engineering 2021, sales 2023) and are not the norm.

## Approval requirements for offers

Offers within band require **hiring manager** approval, with the HR Business Partner confirming band placement before the offer is extended. The HRBP is the final check that the offer falls within the published band for the level and geography. No external offer is extended without the HRBP's sign-off in Workday.

Offers above band require **VP + CHRO** approval. "Above band" means above the published ceiling for the level and geography after all standard adjustments. The VP sponsor must articulate why the candidate justifies a placement no other candidate at that level has received and why the candidate cannot be hired at the next level up instead (where the higher comp would be within-band).

Counter-offers to internal employees considering external offers are not made within band as a matter of policy. If retention is at risk and the employee is correctly placed within their band, the conversation is about role, growth, and engagement, not pay. Where a market correction is genuinely needed across a cohort, it is handled as a comp-cycle adjustment, not a one-employee counter.

## Out-of-band exceptions and process

An out-of-band placement is a deliberate exception, not a workaround for a too-tight band. The exception process requires:

1. **Written justification** from the hiring manager and VP, addressing why within-band placement is not viable.
2. **Comparator analysis** from the HRBP, identifying other employees at the same level and the gap that would be created.
3. **CHRO review and approval** in all cases above the band ceiling.
4. **Board Compensation Committee approval** for any L7 placement more than 15% above band, or any placement that would make the employee one of the company's three highest-paid named-executive officers under Section 16 reporting rules.

Out-of-band placements are tracked in a register reviewed by the Compensation Committee quarterly. A growing register is a signal that the bands themselves need to be re-calibrated.

## Equity refresh policy

The company runs an annual equity refresh cycle aligned with the performance cycle. Refresh sizes are determined by level and by performance rating, with target refresh grants set as a percentage of the new-hire grant for the level. Refresh grants vest over four years from the grant date with no cliff (cliffs apply only to new-hire grants).

Off-cycle equity refreshes — retention grants outside the annual cycle — require **VP + CHRO** approval at all levels. They are used sparingly and primarily in two scenarios: (1) employees newly promoted to a level whose existing grant is materially below the new level's refresh expectation, and (2) employees whose original grant is fully vested and whose role makes a retention case compelling.

## Currency and geo-adjustment

Bands are anchored in the US dollar primary-market range and derived for all other geographies through a published geo-adjustment factor. The factor reflects market pay relative to the primary market, not cost of living. Indicative current factors: London 0.85; Berlin 0.75; Paris 0.78; São Paulo 0.50; Singapore 0.80; remote-Brazil 0.45; remote-Argentina 0.40. The factor is reviewed annually with the band refresh and updated as market conditions warrant.

Pay is denominated in the local currency of employment. Employees moving between countries are re-banded into the destination geography on the effective date of relocation; the move does not preserve the origin-country pay level if it would now be out of band in the destination.

## Annual review cycle

The annual compensation cycle runs February through April. Managers submit recommendations for base, variable target, and equity refresh in February; HR calibrates across functions through March; the cycle closes with manager communication conversations in early April and effective dates of April 1 for cash compensation and April 15 for equity grants. The cycle is the primary mechanism by which most compensation changes happen; out-of-cycle changes are exceptions governed by the *Job Change Approval Matrix*.
