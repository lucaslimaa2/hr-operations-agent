# Contractor to Full-Time Employee Conversion

## Eligibility criteria

A contractor becomes eligible for conversion to full-time employment when three conditions are met. First, the work the contractor performs is sustained and ongoing rather than project-bounded — that is, the company expects the work to continue indefinitely beyond the contractor's current engagement. Second, the receiving team has open headcount approved in the current operating plan, or the conversion is being made in lieu of opening a new requisition that would otherwise be filed. Third, the contractor has performed at a level the hiring manager would be willing to hire externally; conversion is not used as a path to extend an underperforming engagement.

Tenure as a contractor is not an automatic trigger and does not create an entitlement to conversion. Long contractor tenures without conversion are, however, a source of legal risk in several jurisdictions (see country-specific notes), and the People team conducts a quarterly review to flag any contractor relationship exceeding 12 months for either conversion or formal end-of-engagement.

Conversion is not the only path for a contractor whose engagement should continue. Statement-of-work renewal, agency-of-record placement (via a global Employer of Record where the company has no entity in the contractor's country), and structured project work are all valid alternatives evaluated case by case.

## Required approvals

A conversion is a hiring decision and follows the standard hiring approval path for the target level, with two additional approvers layered in. The hiring manager initiates the request through Workday with a proposed level, compensation package, and start date. The standard approval chain for the target level (per the *Job Change Approval Matrix*) applies. In addition, **Finance** signs off to confirm headcount availability and budget impact, and **Legal** signs off to confirm there is no misclassification exposure from the prior contractor period. For conversions in Brazil, Germany, and France, Legal's review is non-discretionary and may not be waived.

The contractor is not informed of the conversion offer until all approvals are complete and the formal offer letter has been issued. Pre-offer conversations confirming interest are permitted but must not contain specific compensation figures or a start date.

## Timeline

A typical conversion takes **two to four weeks** from initial request to first day as an employee. The breakdown:

- Days 1–3: Hiring manager submits the request; HRBP confirms completeness.
- Days 4–10: Approval chain routes through manager, skip-level, HRBP, Finance, and Legal.
- Days 11–14: Formal offer letter generated and reviewed; background-check and employment-eligibility verification initiated (these run in parallel for contractors who have not previously cleared them).
- Days 15–20: Contractor accepts offer; existing contracting arrangement is wound down with final invoice issued; new-hire onboarding scheduled.
- Day 21: Start date as a full-time employee, with day one of new probation period (where applicable).

Cross-country conversions, conversions into countries where the company has no operating entity (requiring EOR setup), and conversions in jurisdictions with mandatory works-council consultation may take longer.

## What changes for the worker

The change from contractor to employee is substantive and reaches well beyond the change in title. The worker should expect the following:

**Compensation structure.** Contractor day-rates or monthly invoices are replaced by employee base salary, eligibility for the company's variable compensation plan (annual bonus or commission, depending on role), and a per-level equity grant per the *Compensation Bands* policy. The total cash compensation as an employee is typically lower than the contractor invoice rate, because the contractor rate must cover the worker's own taxes, social contributions, and lack of benefits. The total-cost-to-company is typically comparable.

**Benefits.** The worker becomes eligible for company health insurance, retirement plan contributions, paid time off, parental leave, learning-and-development allowances, and any other benefits in force in their country of employment. Benefits start on day one of employment unless the local plan has a waiting period.

**Equity.** A new-hire equity grant is issued at the level appropriate to the role, on the standard four-year vesting schedule with a one-year cliff. Prior contractor tenure does not accelerate the cliff.

**Employment type and contract.** The contractor agreement is terminated and replaced by an employment contract under the laws of the country where the worker resides and works. The employment relationship carries statutory protections that the contractor relationship did not — notice periods, severance entitlement under local law, anti-discrimination protections, and so on.

**Tax treatment.** The worker stops invoicing the company and stops handling their own withholding. Income tax and social contributions are withheld at source by the company through local payroll. The worker should expect to consult their personal tax advisor about the transition, particularly if mid-tax-year, and about any business expenses, equipment depreciation, or VAT registrations they were claiming as a contractor that no longer apply.

## What changes for the company

The company assumes the full set of employer obligations under the controlling jurisdiction's labor law from day one of employment. This includes payroll tax registration and withholding, social-security and pension contributions, paid leave accrual, occupational accident insurance, and any statutory severance reserves that local law requires the company to maintain.

Conversion also creates a documentation record that becomes important if the prior contractor relationship is ever audited or litigated. The Legal review at the approval stage produces a memo to file analyzing the prior relationship against the local employment-test criteria and confirming whether the company believes the contractor was properly classified during the prior period. This memo is retained for the statutory limitations period in the jurisdiction.

## Country-specific notes

### Brazil (BR)

PJ-to-CLT conversion is the most consequential employment-type change in the company's portfolio and the one with the highest residual risk. From the date of conversion, the full set of CLT obligations applies: FGTS deposits at 8% of monthly salary into the employee's FGTS account, 13º salário (Christmas bonus paid in two installments), 30 days of annual paid leave with the constitutional 1/3 premium, aviso prévio scaling per Lei 12.506/2011 if and when the relationship later ends, and all the associated payroll-tax obligations (INSS employer contribution, etc.).

The residual risk is the **vínculo empregatício re-classification** of the pre-conversion contractor period. If, during the PJ phase, all four elements of CLT Art. 3º were present — personal performance, non-eventuality, paid work, and subordination — the Justiça do Trabalho may treat the entire PJ period as employment retroactively, with the company liable for back-payment of FGTS for the full period, retroactive 13ºs, retroactive férias + 1/3, and INSS contributions, plus the 40% FGTS multa if the relationship later ends without cause. Statute of limitations is five years of back-claims and two years to file after the relationship ends. Legal's pre-conversion memo evaluates this exposure and the company makes an informed decision whether to proceed, settle the prior period, or restructure.

### Germany (DE)

A new employment contract is issued and a **new probation period (Probezeit) of up to six months** begins on day one of employment. The Kündigungsschutzgesetz protection clock starts fresh at day one of employment and reaches the six-month threshold on day 180 of employment. Prior contractor tenure does not count toward the KSchG six-month threshold and does not count toward any tenure-based notice scaling under BGB §622. The worker's effective protection level under German law on day one of employment is materially lower than it was on the last day of contracting, where the lack of statutory protection was offset by the contractor's commercial freedom. This is communicated explicitly in the offer letter and the pre-signing conversation.

Where a Betriebsrat exists, BetrVG §99 consultation on the hiring is required and runs in parallel with the offer process.

### France (FR)

The conversion produces a new contrat à durée indéterminée (CDI), and the worker's previous tenure as an independent contractor does **not** count toward the calculation of the indemnité de licenciement should the employment later end. The eight-month tenure threshold for indemnité eligibility (Code du travail Art. L1234-9) starts on day one of employment, as does the ancienneté calculation for any future severance.

If the contractor was previously an auto-entrepreneur invoicing primarily or exclusively the company, French courts and URSSAF may apply the requalification doctrine — comparable to the Brazilian vínculo empregatício analysis — and treat the prior period as disguised employment with retroactive social-charge liability. Legal's pre-conversion memo addresses this exposure. Where a CSE exists, consultation obligations on the hiring may apply depending on company size.
